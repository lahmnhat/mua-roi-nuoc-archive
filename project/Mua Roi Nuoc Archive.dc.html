Section II — Origins

Born of Flooded Fields

Est. 11th century · Red River Delta

Múa rối nước — “puppets that dance on water” — is the only puppet theatre in the world whose stage is the surface of a pond.

It rose from the flooded paddies of the Red River Delta around the eleventh century, in villages where the monsoon decided everything. When the fields drowned, the farmers performed on them: the disaster itself became the stage.

A water pavilion — the thủy đình — stands over the communal pond. Behind a split-bamboo screen, puppeteers wade chest-deep, driving lacquered figures with rods and strings hidden beneath the surface. The water conceals the machinery, carries the music, and lets wooden dragons breathe smoke and spit rain.

The art belonged to guilds — phường rối — and to no one else. Mechanisms were hereditary secrets, sworn at the communal house. That secrecy preserved the craft for nine centuries, and now endangers it: when a guild dies, its knots die with it. This archive exists so that they will not.

Section VIII — Chronology

A Thousand Years on Water

Ledger of dates · c. 1010 — 2026
c. 1010

Art of the Delta Courts

Under the Lý dynasty, water puppetry moves between village ponds and royal entertainments in the Red River Delta — the only puppet tradition on earth performed on water.

1121

The Stone Record

The Sùng Thiện Diên Linh stele at Đọi Sơn pagoda describes puppet scenes and a golden tortoise performed on the river for the king's birthday — the art's earliest surviving written record.

15th–19th c.

The Secret Guilds

Village guilds — phường rối — guard their mechanisms as hereditary secrets, sworn at the communal house. Techniques pass from father to son; rival villages never share a knot.

1945–54

The Silent Ponds

War empties the villages and the ponds fall silent. Puppets are crated, buried, lost; some guilds never perform again.

1969

A National Stage

The Thăng Long Water Puppet Theatre is founded in Hà Nội, moving the village pond into a permanent city auditorium.

1984

The World Audience

First major international tours. Foreign stages are flooded to specification, and múa rối nước becomes Việt Nam's most travelled traditional art.

1990s

Return to the Villages

Village troupes such as Đào Thục restore their thủy đình and reopen training. Tourism funds the craft — and changes it.

2026

This Archive

Scanning, recording, and transcribing begin. Sixty-four records are opened; most stand ready for the materials the villages will add.

An archive is a promise made to the future.
